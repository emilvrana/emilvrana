The Compound Cost of Unreliable Agents

May 29, 2026 · Emil Vrána

Your agent works 98% of the time. That's pretty good, right?

Not in production. Not even close.

The Math Is Ugly

A single agent step with 98% accuracy sounds fine. But agents don't run one step — they run pipelines. And pipelines multiply failure:

  • 5-step pipeline at 98% per step: 0.98⁵ = 90.4% success rate
  • 10 steps: 81.7%
  • 20 steps: 66.8%

A "98% reliable" agent in a 20-step workflow fails one out of every three runs. That's not a niche case — most real agent workflows involve 10-20 discrete decisions.

Now add human cost. Every failure requires:

  1. Detection (monitoring, alerting, or worse — a customer complaint)
  2. Diagnosis (logs, traces, reproduction)
  3. Remediation (manual fix, re-run, data correction)

One failure in a pipeline can cost 30-60 minutes of human time. At 33% failure rate, you're looking at 3-6 hours of human work per 10 pipeline runs. The "fully automated" system now needs a human attendant.

The Three Failure Modes

Agent unreliability isn't one thing. It's three:

Silent failures. The agent completes without error, but the output is wrong. These are the most expensive because they propagate downstream. Your RAG pipeline returns plausible-sounding nonsense. Your data pipeline "succeeds" with corrupted records. Nobody notices until the quarterly report is wrong.

Degraded performance. The agent works, but slower or with lower quality. Maybe it's hitting rate limits and falling back to a cheaper model. Maybe context window pressure is causing shortcut reasoning. The system doesn't fail — it just gets worse, gradually, invisibly.

Cascading failures. One step fails, and downstream steps operate on bad input. The retry pattern makes this worse: if step 3 fails and you retry from step 3, you're retrying with the same bad state that caused the failure. The error propagates, compounds, and eventually the entire pipeline produces garbage.

Why "Just Add Retries" Makes It Worse

I wrote about the retry trap before. The short version: most agent failures aren't transient. Retrying a bad prompt with the same context gives you the same bad answer, just slower and more expensive.

In compound failure scenarios, retries are actively harmful:

  • They mask the root cause (the failure "goes away" on retry #3, but for the wrong reason)
  • They multiply cost (3 retries × original cost = 4× for the same result)
  • They delay detection (retry logic adds latency before alerting)

What Actually Works

Validation gates. Every pipeline step should have an output schema and assertions. Not just "did it return JSON?" but "does this output make sense given the input?" A validation gate that catches 80% of silent failures reduces your effective failure rate dramatically.

Idempotent steps. Design each step so it can be safely re-run. This means no side effects until the final step, or side effects that are safe to repeat. When a step fails, you can retry just that step — not the whole pipeline.

Budget limits. Set hard ceilings on cost, latency, and retry count per pipeline run. When you hit a ceiling, fail loud and fail fast. A known failure is infinitely cheaper than an unknown one.

Observability at every step. Agent evaluation isn't just for development. Production monitoring with the same metrics you use in eval — accuracy, latency, cost per step — catches degradation before it compounds.

The Business Case

Here's what 2% unreliability actually costs in a real pipeline:

MetricValue
Pipeline steps15
Per-step reliability98%
Pipeline success rate73.9%
Failed runs per 10026.1
Human fix time per failure45 min
Total human hours per 100 runs19.6

That's half a work week per 100 runs. Scale to 1,000 runs/month and you've hired a full-time employee whose entire job is fixing your "automated" pipeline.

Fix the reliability to 99.5% per step and the math changes:

MetricValue
Per-step reliability99.5%
Pipeline success rate92.8%
Failed runs per 1007.2
Human hours per 100 runs5.4

Still not zero. But 5.4 hours vs. 19.6 hours — that's the difference between a system that needs babysitting and one that mostly runs itself.

The ROI of reliability work compounds too. Every 0.1% improvement in per-step accuracy saves disproportionate time at the pipeline level. The investment in validation, testing, and monitoring pays for itself faster than almost any other optimization.

The Takeaway

"Pretty reliable" isn't a production strategy. In compound systems, small failure rates scale into real costs — human time, customer trust, and eventually, the viability of the automation itself.

Build for 99.5%+. Validate at every step. Monitor what you measure in eval. And remember: the goal isn't zero failures (impossible), it's failures that are detected, contained, and cheap to fix.
